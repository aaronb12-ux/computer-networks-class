## Principles of Reliable Data Transfer (Part 1)

Challenge: How do two distributed entities reliably communicate over a channel which itself is unreliable?

<img width="317" height="151" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-16 at 1 16 12 PM" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/e1a4d654-b187-4d45-a978-bf4c653dcf49" />

The sending process wants to send data to the receiving process through a reliable channel (unidirectional).

The "reliable channel" is an abstraction. Its implementation is in the form of a transport-layer protocol. There are two sides: a sender side of the reliable data transfer protocol and a receiver side. Both sender and receiver communicate with each other over an unreliable channel.

<img width="338" height="255" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-16 at 1 18 43 PM" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/c44baaf7-f3ea-4a17-8288-2090b330ef2b" />

The complexity of the reliable data transfer protocol depends strongly on the characteristics of the unreliable channel (loss, corruption, reordering).

Sender and receiver do not know the "state" of each other (e.g., whether a message was received) unless this is communicated via a message.

---

### Reliable Data Transfer Protocol (rdt): Interfaces

<img width="445" height="236" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-16 at 1 22 56 PM" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/da1562b6-22f9-4a4e-8a14-7498019a3901" />

On the sending side, data is passed down from the application-layer process to the transport layer. The transport layer then adds a header together with the data to create a transport-layer segment and sends that over the unreliable channel to the receiver.

On the receiving side, when a segment arrives, it contains both a header and data. The receiver then delivers the data up to the receiving process at the application layer.

### Reliable Data Transfer: Getting Started

We will:
- incrementally develop sender and receiver sides of the reliable data transfer protocol (rdt)  
- consider only unidirectional data transfer  
  - but control information will flow in both directions  
- use finite state machines (FSMs) to specify sender and receiver  

---

### rdt1.0: Reliable Transfer over a Reliable Channel

Underlying channel is perfectly reliable:
- no bit errors  
- no loss of packets  

Separate FSMs for sender and receiver:
- sender sends data into underlying channel  
- receiver reads data from underlying channel  

---

### rdt2.0: Channel with Bit Errors

Underlying channel may flip bits in a packet:
- checksum is used to detect errors  

The question: how to recover from errors?
- acknowledgements (ACKs): receiver explicitly tells sender that packet was received correctly  
- negative acknowledgements (NAKs): receiver explicitly tells sender that packet had errors  
- sender retransmits packet upon receiving a NAK  

Stop-and-wait:
- sender sends one packet, then waits for receiver response  

---

### rdt2.1: Discussion

Sender:
- sequence number added to packet  
- two sequence numbers (0, 1) are sufficient  
- must check if received ACK/NAK is corrupted  
- has more states:
  - must "remember" whether the expected packet should have sequence number 0 or 1  

Receiver:
- must check if received packet is a duplicate  
- state indicates whether 0 or 1 is the expected sequence number  
- note: receiver cannot know if its last ACK/NAK was received correctly by the sender  

---

### rdt Protocol Mechanisms

- error detection  
- ACKs, NAKs  
- retransmission  
- sequence numbers (for duplicate detection)
