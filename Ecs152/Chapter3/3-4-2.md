## Principles of Reliable Data Transfer (Part 2)

We now extend our reliable data transfer protocol to handle not only **corrupted packets**, but also **lost packets**. Packet loss can occur in both directions:

- **data packets** from sender to receiver
- **ACKs** from receiver back to sender

The **rdt3.0** protocol handles the case of lost packets.

---

### rdt3.0: Channels with Errors and Loss

**New channel assumption:**
- The underlying channel can **corrupt** packets
- The underlying channel can also **lose** packets (both data packets and ACKs)

**Approach:**
- The sender waits a **reasonable amount of time** for an ACK
- If no ACK arrives in time, the sender assumes the packet or ACK was lost and **retransmits**

If a packet or ACK was only **delayed** rather than lost:
- the retransmission may create a **duplicate**
- this is okay because **sequence numbers** already handle duplicates
- the receiver must indicate the **sequence number** of the packet being acknowledged

## Performance of rdt3.0 (Stop-and-Wait)

**Sender utilization** = fraction of time the sender is actively transmitting

### Example
- **Link rate:** 1 Gbps
- **Propagation delay:** 15 ms
- **Packet size:** 8000 bits

**Time to transmit packet into the channel:**

\[
D_{trans} = \frac{L}{R} = \frac{8000 \text{ bits}}{10^9 \text{ bits/sec}} = 8 \text{ microseconds}
\]

### rdt3.0: Stop-and-Wait Operation

<img width="527" height="213" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-16 at 2 56 26 PM" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/d216d554-1eed-42e3-9c4a-8b362f7f0211" />

The amount of time from when the sender first starts transmitting a bit to when it receives the ACK is used to compute sender utilization:

\[
U_{sender} = \frac{L/R}{RTT + L/R}
\]

### Key Idea
rdt3.0 has **poor performance** under stop-and-wait because the sender spends most of its time waiting.  
The protocol ends up limiting the performance of the underlying channel.

## rdt3.0: Pipelined Protocol Operation

**Pipelining** allows the sender to have multiple **in-flight** packets that have been sent but not yet acknowledged.

This requires:
- a larger range of **sequence numbers**
- **buffering** at the sender and/or receiver

## Go-Back-N (GBN)

### Go-Back-N: Sender

The sender maintains a **window** of up to **N** consecutive transmitted but unacknowledged packets.

- uses a **k-bit sequence number** in each packet header

<img width="561" height="123" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-17 at 2 16 25 PM" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/ac5fa9d8-824b-403c-aacc-92c3ee55fcf5" />

### Go-Back-N Rules
- **Cumulative ACK:** `ACK(n)` acknowledges all packets up to and including sequence number `n`
- When `ACK(n)` is received, the sender moves its window forward so it begins at `n + 1`
- The sender uses **one timer** for the **oldest in-flight packet**
- On `timeout(n)`, the sender retransmits packet `n` **and all higher-numbered packets currently in the window**

---

### Go-Back-N: Receiver

Whenever the receiver gets a packet, it sends back an ACK for the **highest in-order packet received so far**.

**ACK-only approach:**
- always send an ACK for the highest correctly received **in-order** sequence number
- may generate **duplicate ACKs**
- receiver only needs to remember `rcv_base`

**If an out-of-order packet arrives:**
- the receiver may **discard it** (not buffer it), or buffer it depending on implementation
- it re-sends an ACK for the highest in-order packet received so far

## Selective Repeat (SR)

In **Selective Repeat**:

### Receiver
- individually acknowledges each correctly received packet
- buffers packets as needed for eventual **in-order delivery** to the upper layer

### Sender
- times out and retransmits **only specific unacknowledged packets**
- maintains a **separate timer for each unACKed packet**

## Summary

### Reliable Data Transfer Mechanisms
- **error detection** (for example, checksum)
- **ACKs and NAKs**
- **retransmission**
- **sequence numbers** for duplicate detection
- **timeout and retransmission** for lost packets
